Reference

Method and Caveats

What This Is

A staff sketch tool for structuring bonus-density negotiations — order-of-magnitude figures to frame a decision, not a certified appraisal, developer pro forma, or legal determination. Treat the outputs as directional and calibrate the inputs to local data before relying on any single number.

The Numbers, in Plain Terms

Value to developer — the most the bonus units are worth to the developer (their residual profit, slightly discounted): the walk-away ceiling.

Pool opportunity cost — the value of giving up scarce pool units. Near zero when the pool is plentiful; rises as it nears depletion. Applies only on the pool-allocation pathway.

Impact mitigation floor — the development's impact that can lawfully be required as a condition; the most the city can compel. Benefit beyond it is voluntary incentive.

Lawful mitigation baseline — opportunity cost plus impact mitigation: the least public value the deal should return.

Developer cost — what the offered package costs the developer to provide.

Public value offered — the city's plain-dollar cost to provide the offered package (qty × city $/unit × % above code). This is what the verdict compares to the baseline. Category multipliers do not enter this figure — they only rank benefits within a package.

Value ratio — public value per developer dollar. The amount above 1 is the gain from trade.

How the Recommendation Is Reached

The deal is worthwhile when the public value offered meets the lawful mitigation baseline, and feasible when the developer cost stays under the value to developer. The zone of possible agreement runs from the least developer spending that satisfies the city up to the value to developer; the gap is the negotiating room. Fill that room with the highest value-ratio benefits first.

Known Limits — Read Before Relying on It

Plain-dollar verdict. The pass/fail test is apples-to-apples in plain dollars: the package's plain public value (city cost to provide) against the plain-dollar baseline. The category multipliers (resilience 2.5×, etc.) are a ranking tool only — they steer which benefits to prefer within a package, and never inflate the offer on the verdict scale. (Earlier versions weighted the offer before the test; that was removed in v1.1 — see the changelog below.) If a deal now reads tighter than it used to, the tool didn't get stricter — it stopped grading the developer's offer on a curve.

Invented curve shapes. The scarcity exponent and the value-decline factor are reasonable judgment shapes, not empirically derived. Both are editable under Assumptions.

Margin is not a return. The developer margin is applied as a flat percentage of total cost — a sketch proxy for a real 12–15% target IRR. It does not model financing, hold period, or the time value of money, so a developer's analyst will compute feasibility on a different basis. Treat the value to developer as an order-of-magnitude ceiling, not a pro-forma result.

Land is held constant. The value to developer prices only the marginal bonus units; it does not subtract land cost (land is a by-right sunk cost that does not scale with the bonus). Correct for valuing the increment, but it is not a whole-project residual land value and understates the developer's all-in cost.

Double-counting trap. If required mitigation is also entered as a voluntary benefit, it is counted both in the impact floor and in the offered value. Use the "% above code" field to count only the genuinely above-code share.

Calibration. Hard costs, category multipliers, impact figures, and district base/cap densities are placeholders. They need local construction-cost data, a current concurrency/impact study, and the city's adopted priorities before any output is load-bearing.

Auto-zoning is best-effort. Always confirm the district and verify against the official Future Land Use map.

Basis

Impact figures sit within the range of recent Florida mobility/parks/fire/school impact-fee schedules. The resilience weighting is a stand-in for a project-specific benefit-cost ratio (for example, from a FEMA-style avoided-loss analysis), which should replace it where one exists. District categories and densities follow the comprehensive plan; the Large Resort and Town Center categories are under city review through 2026 and should be checked against the current code.

Codified backing. The bonus mechanism itself is in the SPB code: added units come only from a finite density pool — the General Residential Unit "RU" pool of 195 units (Land Development Code §39.18) and the 325-unit transient-lodging pool for the Town Center-2 / Activity Center / Bayou Residential districts (Future Land Use element), both created by the 2007 down-zoning of the Large Resort district across 65.16 acres (−3 du/ac → 195; −5 lodging units/ac → 325). Each allocation is by City Commission ordinance plus conditional use on a discretionary "merit basis" (LDC §4.12(d)), with the units-remaining balance recited in the ordinance (§39.18(c)). The qualifying public benefits are enumerated in §4.12(d)(1)–(9): public parking / multimodal, public beach access, public art, public boat slips, workforce housing, green-building / wellness, Gulf-front access easements, and landscaping above code.

What the code does not set. The SPB code contains no contribution rate, no benefit-valuation formula, and no floor or ceiling on what a developer must offer — the only statutory weighting is Chapter 163 proportionate-share ("in proportion to the impacts caused"), which is the basis for the impact-mitigation floor. The ZOPA bounds in this tool, and the 15–25% contribution / 25–50% capture guardrails cited below, are therefore analytic overlays from the supporting framework, not adopted code; the value-ratio math is a staff sketch over the code's discretionary merit-basis review.

Supporting framework. This tool operationalizes the approach in A Framework for Density Bonus Valuation in St. Pete Beach (Tampa Bay Regional Planning Council, April 2026): density here is an allocation of a finite, non-renewable pool, not a growth incentive, so each bonus unit carries scarcity value, and the negotiation is bounded by a Zone of Possible Agreement between a scarcity-adjusted city floor and a developer walk-away ceiling. That framework documents the legal basis for the floor (Florida proportionate-share statute F.S. §163.3180; the Nollan–Dolan–Koontz nexus and rough-proportionality standards) and the value-capture rationale for the ceiling (Lincoln Institute / ULI), and it frames public benefits across five value layers — construction cost, city replacement-cost premium, lifecycle O&M, resilience / avoided damage, and regional economic impact. This tool intentionally uses a simpler single-value-per-benefit model; the framework’s layered valuation and its 15–25% contribution / 25–50% capture guardrails are documented there for staff who want the fuller rationale.

Sources & benchmarks. The benchmark figures behind this tool trace to published sources, carried on the Assumptions and Benefit-library pages (Source column): construction cost RSMeans and ICC Building Valuation Data; lodging STR / HVS; soft costs and developer margin ULI / CBRE; condo comps Redfin; benefit unit costs from SFRPC (Resilient BCA Database / REBECCA), FEMA (Ecosystem Service Values; BCA Sustainment v13), FDEP, FHWA, WGI, Forward Pinellas, UF Shimberg, and HUD. The framework’s fuller valuation method draws on residual-land-value practice (Keyser Marston; BAE Urban Economics) and value-layer multipliers (NIBS Natural Hazard Mitigation Saves 2019 for resilience; GASB 34 / FHWA lifecycle; FL Statute 255.20 public-procurement premium; BEA / RIMS II economic impact) — those layers inform the benchmarks but are not recomputed here.
